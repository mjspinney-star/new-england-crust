We fired up the pizza oven last weekend even though it was cold enough to see our breath. The oven lit fine, the thermometer climbed like normal — and then the first pie came out with a burnt bottom and a pale top. Classic cold-weather trap, and we walked right into it again.

The stone surface looked ready. It wasn't.

Here is the thing: in cold weather, the display and the actual stone core are not in agreement. The surface can read 900°F while the mass underneath is still cold from sitting outside — and that cold mass pulls heat out of your dough the second it lands. Our fix: add at least 15 to 20 minutes to whatever your normal preheat time is. If you usually wait 25 minutes, wait 40 to 45. Then test with a pinch of flour — it should brown in under 10 seconds. If it doesn't, wait. The thermometer reading is not the whole story.

If you're running propane, there's a second thing: cold tanks lose pressure. The flame looks weaker because liquid propane doesn't vaporize efficiently below 40°F. Keep the tank in a garage or mudroom for 20 minutes before the cook. It makes a real difference and costs nothing.
