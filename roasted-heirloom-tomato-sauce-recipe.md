## It Only Exists for Six Weeks

There's a window every August in New England — maybe six weeks, maybe eight if the season is generous — when farm stands overflow with heirloom tomatoes in colors that look almost fictional. Brandywines the size of softballs. Cherokee Purples with their dusty, burgundy skin. Green Zebras striped like something from a botanical illustration. Sun Golds so sweet they taste more like fruit than vegetable.

We wait for this window all year. And when it arrives, we make this sauce in large batches, use it immediately on pizza, and freeze the rest to carry us through October.

It is not a complicated sauce. It is not a quick sauce. What it is, is the best pizza sauce we've ever made — and the only one that actually tastes like New England summer captured in a jar.

---

## Why Roasting Changes Everything

Most tomato sauces — including perfectly good ones — are built on the stovetop. You cook down canned or fresh tomatoes with garlic and olive oil, season them, and you're done in 20 minutes.

Roasting does something different. When heirloom tomatoes spend 45–60 minutes in a hot oven, several things happen:

- **Moisture evaporates**, concentrating the tomato flavor dramatically
- **Natural sugars caramelize**, adding depth and a subtle sweetness that you can't replicate on a stovetop
- **The edges darken**, contributing a slight bitterness that balances the sweetness perfectly
- **The garlic mellows**, going from sharp and raw to soft and nutty

The result has a complexity that canned tomato sauce — even very good canned tomato sauce — simply can't match. On a pizza fired at 800°F+, it holds together without releasing too much liquid, which means a crisper crust and a cleaner slice.

## What to Look For at the Farm Stand

**[PHOTO: A flat-lay of mixed heirloom tomatoes on a wooden surface — variety of colors and sizes. This is a high-save Pinterest image for the recipe.]**

Not all heirlooms roast equally. Here's what we look for:

**Best for roasting:**
- **Brandywine** — large, meaty, low water content, deep flavor. Our first choice.
- **Cherokee Purple** — rich, almost smoky flavor profile. Excellent blended with Brandywines.
- **San Marzano heirlooms** — if your farm stand carries them, grab every one. They're the classic pizza tomato for a reason.
- **Roma heirlooms** — drier than slicing tomatoes, concentrate beautifully in the oven.

**Use sparingly or avoid for this sauce:**
- **Green Zebra** — great fresh, but the tartness can become sharp when roasted. Add one or two for brightness, not as a base.
- **Sun Gold or other cherry varieties** — very sweet, which can make the sauce one-dimensional. A handful is fine; a pint is too much.

The honest answer is: buy whatever looks best at your farm stand. A mix of two or three varieties produces a more interesting sauce than any single type alone.

## The Recipe

**Yield:** About 2 cups of sauce (enough for 4–6 pizzas)
**Active time:** 15 minutes
**Roasting time:** 50–60 minutes
**Total time:** About 1 hour 15 minutes

### Ingredients

- 3 lbs mixed heirloom tomatoes (roughly 6–8 medium tomatoes)
- 6 cloves garlic, unpeeled
- 3 tablespoons good olive oil, plus more for finishing
- 1 teaspoon kosher salt, plus more to taste
- ½ teaspoon black pepper
- 1 teaspoon sugar (optional — taste your tomatoes first; sweet varieties won't need it)
- Small handful of fresh basil leaves
- ¼ teaspoon red chili flakes (optional)

### Equipment

- Large rimmed baking sheet
- Parchment paper or aluminum foil
- Blender or food processor (or an immersion blender)
- Fine mesh strainer (optional, for a smoother sauce)

---

### Instructions

**Step 1: Preheat and prep**
Preheat your oven to 400°F. Line a large rimmed baking sheet with parchment paper or foil — the tomatoes will release liquid and caramelize, and you want that fond to stay on the pan, not baked permanently onto it.

**Step 2: Prep the tomatoes**
Core the tomatoes and cut them in half through the equator (not stem to bottom). For large Brandywines, cut into quarters. Arrange them cut-side up on the baking sheet in a single layer. Tuck the unpeeled garlic cloves in between the tomatoes.

**Step 3: Season and roast**
Drizzle the olive oil evenly over the tomatoes. Sprinkle with salt and pepper. Roast at 400°F for 50–60 minutes, until the tomatoes are deeply caramelized around the edges, slightly collapsed, and the liquid on the pan has reduced and darkened. The edges should look almost too dark — that's where the flavor is.

**[PHOTO: Mid-roast shot — tomatoes collapsing, edges beginning to caramelize. Shows the transformation happening in the oven.]**

**Step 4: Cool slightly, then squeeze the garlic**
Remove the pan from the oven and let everything cool for 10 minutes. Squeeze the roasted garlic out of its skins directly onto the pan — it should slide out easily and be soft and golden. Discard the skins.

**Step 5: Blend**
Transfer everything from the pan — tomatoes, garlic, and all the pan juices — into a blender or food processor. Add the fresh basil. Pulse until you reach your preferred consistency. We like ours with a little texture remaining, not completely smooth. If you want a very smooth sauce, pass it through a fine mesh strainer after blending.

Taste and adjust: add more salt, a pinch of sugar if the tomatoes were tart, or chili flakes if you want heat.

**Step 6: Finish**
Drizzle with a little additional olive oil before using or storing. The sauce should be thick enough to coat a spoon — if it seems thin, return it to a small saucepan and simmer for 5–10 minutes to reduce further.

## How to Use It

**On pizza:** Use about 3–4 tablespoons per 12-inch pizza. Less than you think. Spread it thin — the flavor is concentrated and a heavy hand will make the crust soggy.

**On white pizzas:** Swirl a small amount alongside olive oil as a base instead of using it as the primary sauce. The contrast between the roasted tomato and the white base is excellent.

**As a dipping sauce:** Serve warm alongside a focaccia or a par-baked crust with a drizzle of olive oil. It disappears immediately.

---

## Storage and Freezing

**Refrigerator:** Keeps well for up to 5 days in an airtight container.

**Freezer:** This is where the real value is. Freeze in ½ cup portions in zip-lock bags or silicone ice cube trays. Once frozen, transfer to a larger freezer bag. Keeps for up to 3 months. Thaw overnight in the fridge or for a few hours at room temperature.

We make 4–6 batches in August and freeze most of it. By November, when New England farm stands are long closed, we're still making pizza with sauce that tastes like summer.

## A Note on Canned Tomatoes in the Off-Season

We won't pretend heirloom tomatoes are available year-round in New England — they're not. From October through July, we use high-quality canned San Marzano tomatoes for our pizza sauce. It's a different sauce and a very good one.

But this roasted heirloom version? It's the reason we get a little impatient every July, watching the farm stands and waiting for the Brandywines to arrive.

## Frequently Asked Questions

**Can I use regular (non-heirloom) tomatoes?**
Yes. Roma tomatoes roast particularly well and are available year-round. The sauce will be good. It won't have the complexity of a mixed heirloom batch, but it's a solid everyday option.

**Do I have to peel the tomatoes?**
No — roasting softens the skin enough that it blends smoothly. If you prefer a very refined texture, you can peel after roasting (the skins slip off easily once cooked), but we don't bother.

**Can I add onion?**
Yes. Half a yellow onion, cut into wedges and roasted alongside the tomatoes, adds sweetness and body. We sometimes include it, sometimes don't.

**My sauce is too thin — what happened?**
A few possible causes: the tomatoes had high water content, the pan was too crowded (tomatoes steam instead of roast when packed tightly), or the roasting time was too short. Fix it on the stovetop — simmer uncovered in a small saucepan until it reaches the consistency you want.

**Can I make this with a wood-fired oven?**
Yes, and it's excellent. Roast the tomatoes in a cast iron pan or on a sheet pan in your outdoor oven at a lower heat setting (around 400–450°F). The slight smoke character from the wood adds another layer of flavor.
